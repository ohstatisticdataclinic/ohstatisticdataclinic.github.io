Understanding Tables, Data, and Statistics: Progression-Free Survival (PFS)

Press Play or Swipe left to begin

What is PFS?

Progression-Free Survival (PFS) is a critical endpoint in oncology trials. It measures the time from randomization until objective tumor progression or death from any cause.

The Two Outcomes:

  • Event: The patient experiences disease progression (via imaging/scans) or dies.
  • Censoring: The patient has not experienced an event by the time of data analysis, is lost to follow-up, or starts a new anti-cancer therapy. We know they were progression-free up until their last scan.

PFS is often preferred over Overall Survival (OS) because it requires fewer patients and less time to reach statistical significance, and is not confounded by subsequent therapies.

Table Interpretation

Reading a Time-to-Event table requires understanding specific statistical notations.

  • "+" (Plus Sign): Often seen next to max times (e.g., 24.5+). It indicates a censored observation. The patient was progression-free at 24.5 months, but their ultimate outcome is unknown.
  • NR (Not Reached): The Kaplan-Meier curve has not dropped below the relevant threshold (e.g., 50% for median). The data is too immature to calculate it.
The Two Denominators Trap:
When calculating percentages for Reasons for Censoring, what is the denominator? Is it the Total N (100) or the Total Censored (60)? Always check the footnotes. Regulatory bodies often prefer Total N to see the whole trial context.

Data Structure (CDISC)

To generate these statistics, programmers map clinical data into standardized datasets.

ADSL (Subject Level)

Contains one record per patient. Crucial variables include Treatment Arm (TRT01P) and Population flags (FASFL, SAFFL) which define the denominators.

ADEFFTTE (Time-to-Event)

Contains multiple endpoints per patient. For PFS:

  • PARAMCD = 'PFS'
  • AVAL = Analysis Value (Time in months/days)
  • CNSR = Censor flag (0 = Event, 1 = Censored)
  • EVNTDESC = Description of what happened (Progression, Death, Clinical Cutoff)

Master's-Level Research

Beyond basic tables, researchers investigate complex questions using PFS data:

  1. Comparative Efficacy in Subgroups: Does the PFS benefit hold true across specific biomarker subgroups (e.g., PD-L1 positive vs negative)? Requires Cox Proportional Hazards modeling with interaction terms.
  2. Early PFS as a Surrogate: Can 6-month landmark PFS accurately predict Overall Survival (OS) at 24 months? This speeds up future trial designs.
  3. Impact of Censoring Patterns: Does informative censoring (e.g., patients dropping out specifically due to drug toxicity before progression) bias the Kaplan-Meier estimates?

Statistical Methods

Standard Kaplan-Meier

Used to estimate the PFS quartiles (25th, Median, 75th) and landmark rates. It handles varying follow-up times and censoring.

  • Event = 1 (Progression/Death)
  • Censored = 0 (No event)

Reverse Kaplan-Meier

Used specifically to calculate Median Follow-up maturity. It tells us how long patients have been observed.

  • We mathematically "flip" the status indicator.
  • Censored (no event) becomes the "Event" (1).
  • Actual Events (Progression/Death) become "Censored" (0).

Programming Code

How statisticians generate the numbers.

SAS (PROC LIFETEST)

proc lifetest data=adefftte plots=survival;
  time aval * cnsr(1); /* 1 is censored */
  strata trt01p;
  where paramcd='PFS';
run;

R (survival package)

library(survival)
# cnsr: 0=Event, 1=Censored. 
# Surv() expects Event=1, Censor=0
status <- ifelse(adefftte$cnsr == 0, 1, 0)

km_fit <- survfit(
  Surv(aval, status) ~ trt01p, 
  data = adefftte
)
print(km_fit)

QC & RA Submission Checklist

Before submitting to the FDA or EMA, ensure the following are validated:

  • Denominator Check: Do the N's in the table header exactly match the Intent-to-Treat (ITT) or Full Analysis Set (FAS) population in ADSL?
  • Censoring Hierarchy: Did you apply the correct FDA/EMA censoring rules? (e.g., If a patient misses two consecutive scans and then progresses, they are usually censored at the last adequate scan).
  • Date Imputation: Are partial dates for death or scans imputed according to the Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP)?
  • Proportional Hazards: Have you tested the assumption? If curves cross, the Hazard Ratio (HR) might be invalid.
