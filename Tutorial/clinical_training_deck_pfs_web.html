Progression-Free Survival (PFS)

A Clinical & Statistical Guide for Researchers

Understanding Tables, Data, and Statistics

What is PFS?

Definition: The time from randomization (or start of treatment) until objective tumor progression or death from any cause, whichever occurs first.

The Two Outcomes

  • Events: The patient experiences disease progression (verified by scans, e.g., RECIST criteria) or dies.
  • Censoring: The event has not occurred by the time of analysis, or the patient is lost to follow-up. We know they were progression-free up to a certain date, but not after.

Clinical Importance

PFS is often preferred over Overall Survival (OS) in modern trials because it isolates the effect of the study drug from subsequent therapies. It requires fewer patients and less time to reach data maturity compared to OS.

Example PFS Table

A standard summary table comparing Generic Drug A to a Control arm.

Parameter Drug A (N=100) Control (N=100)
Events, n (%) 45 (45%) 70 (70%)
Censored, n (%) 55 (55%) 30 (30%)
  Reason: Ongoing in study 40 15
  Reason: Lost to follow-up 15 15
Median PFS (Months) [95% CI] 14.2 [12.1, 18.5] 8.5 [6.2, 10.1]
Min, Max (Months) 0.5, 24.0+ 0.2, 22.5+
Quartiles (25%, Median, 75%) 8.1, 14.2, NR 4.0, 8.5, 12.0
6-Month Landmark PFS % 82% 60%
12-Month Landmark PFS % 65% 35%

Interpreting the Table Elements

  • The "+" Symbol (e.g., 24.0+): Indicates a censored observation. The patient was progression-free at 24 months, but their final outcome is unknown beyond that point.
  • NR (Not Reached): The median or quartile cannot be calculated because not enough events have occurred in the cohort. If the survival curve hasn't dropped below 50%, the median is NR.
  • Landmark Percentages: The probability of surviving without progression at a specific time point (e.g., 6 or 12 months), derived from the Kaplan-Meier curve, not simple division.
The "Two Denominators" Trap:
When calculating event rates, the denominator is usually the Full Analysis Set (FAS) / Intention-to-Treat (ITT) population. However, when analyzing reasons for censoring, the denominator is strictly the number of censored patients, not the total N.

Data Collection & Structure

To calculate PFS, clinical data managers collect specific dates: Randomization Date, Date of Progression (from radiological scans like CT/MRI), Date of Death, and Date of Last Contact.

CDISC Data Standards

In standardized clinical databases, PFS is derived using specific datasets:

  • ADSL (Subject Level Analysis Dataset): Contains baseline demographics, treatment arm, and randomization date.
  • ADTTE / ADEFFTTE (Time to Event Dataset): The core dataset for survival analysis. Contains the calculated duration (AVAL) and the censoring flag (CNSR).

Key Variables in ADTTE

  • AVAL: Analysis Value (the time duration, e.g., in months).
  • CNSR: Censor Flag (0 = Event occurred, 1 = Censored). Note that in statistics, 0 usually means censored, but CDISC standardizes 0 as the event.

Master's Level Research Questions

As a medical researcher, formulating the right question dictates the statistical approach. Here are three standard PFS research angles:

  1. Comparative Efficacy:
    "Does Drug A significantly prolong median PFS compared to Standard of Care in patients with advanced solid tumors?" (Requires Kaplan-Meier and Log-rank test).
  2. Landmark Survival & Durability:
    "What is the probability of patients remaining progression-free at 12 and 24 months on the novel therapy?" (Focuses on survival probabilities at specific timepoints).
  3. Data Maturity & Follow-up Adequacy:
    "Is the current follow-up time sufficient to draw definitive conclusions about the PFS benefit?" (Requires calculating Median Follow-up using Reverse Kaplan-Meier).

Statistical Tests for PFS

  • Kaplan-Meier (KM) Method: A non-parametric statistic used to estimate the survival function from lifetime data. It handles censored data perfectly.
  • Log-Rank Test: A hypothesis test to compare the survival distributions of two samples (e.g., Drug A vs Control). It tests the null hypothesis that there is no difference between the populations.
  • Cox Proportional Hazards Model: A regression model used to investigate the association between survival time and one or more predictor variables. It calculates the Hazard Ratio (HR). An HR < 1 indicates a lower risk of progression/death in the treatment group.
Reverse Kaplan-Meier: Used to calculate the Median Follow-up Time. You swap the event and censor indicators (Events become 0, Censored become 1). This tells you how long patients are being observed, regardless of progression.

Programming Code Snippets

How statisticians generate PFS outputs in standard software:

SAS (PROC LIFETEST)

proc lifetest data=adtte plots=survival;
  time aval * cnsr(1); /* cnsr=1 means censored */
  strata trt01p;       /* Treatment group */
run;

R (survival package)

library(survival)
# Status: 1=censored, 2=event (or 0/1 depending on setup)
km_fit <- survfit(Surv(time_months, event_status) ~ treatment, data=adtte)
plot(km_fit, col=c("blue", "red"))
survdiff(Surv(time_months, event_status) ~ treatment, data=adtte) # Log-rank

QC & Regulatory (RA) Checklist

When reviewing PFS data for a thesis or publication, verify these common pitfalls:

  • FDA/EMA Censoring Rules: Did a patient start a new anti-cancer therapy before progressing? Regulatory agencies often require censoring the patient at the date of the last adequate tumor assessment prior to the new therapy.
  • Missing Tumor Assessments: If a patient misses two consecutive scans and then progresses, the progression date is often censored at the last available scan, rather than counting as an event.
  • Denominator Checks: Ensure the N for safety analyses vs efficacy analyses are distinct. PFS is almost always analyzed on the Intent-to-Treat (ITT) / Full Analysis Set (FAS).
  • Proportional Hazards Assumption: Before reporting a Cox Hazard Ratio, ensure the survival curves don't cross early on. If they cross, the proportional hazards assumption is violated.
